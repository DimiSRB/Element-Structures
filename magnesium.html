MAGNESIUM

ABOUT MAGNESIUM

Magnesium, symbol Mg, is a lightweight metal with atomic number 12. A member of the alkaline earth metals in Group 2, Magnesium is essential both industrially and biologically — it’s the metal that keeps engines light and chlorophyll molecules functioning in plants.

Magnesium has an atomic mass of about 24.31 u and an electron configuration of [Ne] 3s². It is in Period 3 and typically forms stable Mg²⁺ ions by losing two electrons. Though reactive, it is more stable than its Group 1 neighbors and burns with a brilliant white flame.

With a melting point of 650°C (1202°F) and a boiling point of 1090°C (1994°F), Magnesium is solid at room temperature and has a relatively low density of 1.738 g/cm³. It’s often alloyed with aluminum to create lightweight structural materials.

Magnesium is abundant in Earth's crust and is found in minerals like dolomite and magnesite, as well as in seawater. It plays a vital role in human health — being involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions in the body — and is found in green leafy vegetables, nuts, and grains.

The element was first isolated by Sir Humphry Davy in 1808. The name “Magnesium” is derived from Magnesia, a region in Thessaly, Greece, where the mineral magnesite was found.

Fun fact: Magnesium is the central element in the chlorophyll molecule, meaning no photosynthesis — and no plant life — could exist without it.
