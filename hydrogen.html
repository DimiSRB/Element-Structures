HYDROGEN

ABOUT HYDROGEN

Hydrogen — the very first element in the periodic table — is a cornerstone of the universe. With the atomic symbol H and an atomic number of 1, Hydrogen is the lightest and most abundant element known to science.

At standard temperature and pressure, Hydrogen exists as a colorless, odorless gas. It has an exceptionally low density of 0.08988 g/L and a remarkably low melting point of -259.16°C and boiling point of -252.87°C. These extremes help explain why Hydrogen is rarely found as a pure element on Earth, but rather as a component of water (H₂O) and organic compounds.

Its atomic structure is elegantly simple: just one proton and one electron, with an electron configuration of 1s¹. This simplicity makes Hydrogen both scientifically fascinating and chemically reactive, readily forming diatomic molecules (H₂) and participating in countless chemical reactions.

Although Hydrogen sits in Group 1 of the periodic table with the alkali metals, it’s often placed separately due to its unique properties. It shares some similarities with alkali metals, but also with halogens, and its placement continues to be debated among chemists.

Hydrogen has three known isotopes: Protium (¹H), the most common; Deuterium (²H), which contains one neutron; and Tritium (³H), a rare radioactive isotope used in nuclear fusion and research.

Discovered by Henry Cavendish in 1766, Hydrogen was once known as “flammable air” due to its explosive reaction with oxygen. Today, it’s central to numerous scientific fields — from cosmology to clean energy research.

Hydrogen is vital to the universe's structure. It fuels the stars through nuclear fusion, forming heavier elements in a process that powers the cosmos. On Earth, it’s a promising player in the quest for sustainable energy. Hydrogen is used in fuel cells to generate electricity, in ammonia production, rocket propulsion, and in the hydrogenation of fats and oils.

Fun fact: Despite its invisibility, nearly 75% of the universe’s elemental mass is Hydrogen. Every star you see in the night sky is essentially a glowing sphere of Hydrogen undergoing fusion.
